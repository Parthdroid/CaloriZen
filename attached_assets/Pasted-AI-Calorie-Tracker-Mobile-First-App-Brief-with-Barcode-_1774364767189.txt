AI Calorie Tracker — Mobile-First App Brief (with Barcode + Low-Confidence Questions)
Product Vision

Build a mobile-first AI calorie tracking app that helps users log meals in seconds using the fastest input method for the situation:

take a photo for fresh meals
scan a barcode for packaged foods
answer 1–2 quick follow-up questions only when the AI is unsure
review and save with minimal typing

The app should feel like a real phone product: fast, camera-driven, thumb-friendly, and built for daily use. Desktop is optional and secondary.

Core App Promise

A user should be able to:

Open the app on their phone
Tap Scan Meal or Scan Barcode
Capture a meal photo, choose one from gallery, or scan packaged food
Get calories and macros quickly
If AI confidence is low, answer 1–2 short questions
Review the result
Save the meal in seconds
See daily calorie and macro progress immediately

The app should optimize for speed first, while improving accuracy only when needed.

Main Inputs
1. Photo Scan

Best for:

home-cooked meals
restaurant food
mixed plates
Indian meals
fresh food without packaging

Flow:

take photo or choose from gallery
AI detects foods and portions
if confidence is high, show result directly

2. Barcode Scan

Best for:

packaged snacks
drinks

Flow:

Barcode should be treated as a core feature, not a later add-on, because packaged food logging is one of the fastest and most reliable user flows.

3. Manual Entry

Fallback for:

AI failure
unclear photos
homemade meals with many hidden ingredients
user preference

Flow:

tap Enter Manually
add food item(s)
choose serving size
save
Smart Accuracy Flow

The AI should not pretend to know everything.
If confidence is low, it should ask 1–2 short questions before finalizing calories.

Rule
High confidence → show result immediately
Low confidence → ask 1–2 questions, then update result
Very low confidence / failed analysis → suggest manual entry
Examples of clarification questions

The app should ask only the most useful question(s), such as:

“Was this full-fat paneer or tofu?”
“How many rotis were there?”
“Was this one bowl or two bowls of rice?”
“Was the drink regular or sugar-free?”
“Was this fried or grilled?”
“Is this chicken or soya?”
“Roughly how large was the serving: small, medium, or large?”
Important constraints
maximum 2 questions
use buttons/chips, not long typing
skip questions if confidence is already good
do not make every meal feel slow

This makes the app feel smart without becoming annoying.

Low-Confidence Question Logic

The AI should return:

detected items
estimated portions
calories/macros
confidence score
optional clarification prompts
Example response behavior

If the AI sees a curry plate with rice and bread but is unsure:

detect likely items
assign lower confidence
ask:
“How many rotis were there?”
“Was the rice one bowl or two?”

Then recompute totals.

Good design rule

Questions should only appear if they can materially improve calorie accuracy.

Do not ask vague questions like:

“Can you explain your meal?”
“Tell me more”

Ask only structured, easy-answer questions.

Accuracy Strategy
High-confidence scenarios
clear packaged products with barcode
single packaged drinks/snacks
obvious simple meals
Medium-confidence scenarios
simple plates with visible items
common foods with recognizable portions
Low-confidence scenarios
curries
mixed bowls
gravies
fried assortments
hidden ingredients
multi-item Indian thali
foods partly covered or blurred

For low-confidence meals, the app should use the question step instead of giving false certainty.

Best UI patterns:

Avoid:

Example answer formats:
Small / Medium / Large
1 / 2 / 3 rotis
Fried / Grilled
Regular / Sugar-free

Final app idea, rewritten cleanly

Build a mobile-first AI calorie counting app designed for fast daily food logging on phones. Users can log meals by taking a photo of food or scanning a barcode on packaged products. The app should estimate calories, protein, carbs, and fat, then let users quickly review and edit results before saving. When the AI is uncertain, it should ask 1–2 short multiple-choice questions to improve accuracy, such as portion size, food type, or cooking style. The overall experience should feel fast, simple, and native to mobile, with bottom-tab navigation, large touch targets, quick scan flows, and instant daily progress tracking
